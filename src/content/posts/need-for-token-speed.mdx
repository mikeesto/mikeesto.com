If you've ever browsed an AI routing platform like
[OpenRouter](https://openrouter.ai), you'll notice that every model listed comes
with two critical data points: latency and throughput.

When you send a request to a model, latency is how long you wait before anything
happens. Throughput, measured in tokens per second (TPS), is how fast the tokens
flow once the inference starts [better phrasing needed here overall].

What even is a token?

A token is roughly a word. Not quite a word, not quite a syllable, somewhere in
between. "Unbelievable" might be two or three tokens. "The" is one. As a rough
rule of thumb, 100 tokens ≈ 75 words. If someone says a model does 50 TPS, they
mean about 37–38 words per second. That's faster than almost anyone reads, but
you can still immediately tell the difference between 10 TPS and 200 TPS. [does

So, ultimately, latency and throughput are related, but they're not the same
thing. A model, being offered by a particular provider, could have very low
latency (starts fast) but modest throughput (plods along). Another might make
you wait a beat, then respond almost instantly. Low latency and high throughput
is the dream, but it is easier said than done at scale.

### Throughput is becoming a product

Something is shifting right now in how the industry thinks about inference
throughput. For a long time, the implicit assumption was that intelligence and
speed trade off against each other: smarter models are slower, faster models are
dumber. That's starting to crack - and more interestingly, both OpenAI and
Anthropic seem to have arrived at the same conclusion at almost the same moment.
Speed at the frontier may be a market in and of itself, and people may be
willing to pay (substantially) for it.

Here's what's happened in just the last two weeks. OpenAI released
GPT-5.3-Codex-Spark, a coding model designed to feel near instant. It is running
on hardware from [Cerebras](https://www.cerebras.ai), a chipmaker whose
Wafer-Scale Engine 3 is essentially a single chip the size of a dinner plate,
with 4 trillion transistors, built specifically for low latency inference.
Codex-Spark delivers over 1,000 tokens per second. It's currently in preview for
ChatGPT Pro subscribers, that's the $200/month USD tier, so no, I haven't tried
it yet!

Anthropic meanwhile has been even more explicit about the premium angle. A week
ago they shipped
[Fast Mode for Claude Opus 4.6](https://platform.claude.com/docs/en/build-with-claude/fast-mode).
It's the same model, same weights, same intelligence, just faster. About 2.5x
faster in terms of output tokens per second. And the price for that extra speed
is 6x the standard rate. Standard Opus 4.6 runs $5/$25 per million tokens
(input/output). Fast Mode is $30/$150. If you stack Fast Mode with the new 1M
token long context option, you can end up paying $60/$225 per million tokens —
which is, to put it gently [better phrasing needed], a lot.

Both of these releases tell the same story. OpenAI and Anthropic are betting
that a subset of users will be willing to pay more, a lot more, for the "faster
frontier". How big this market segment is, I genuinely don't know.

And then, slightly further from the mainstream spotlight, we also saw a 25
person startup called [Taalas](https://chatjimmy.ai) unveil a chip they've named
the HC1, which runs Llama 3.1 8B at around 15,000 – 17,000 tokens per second.
That's not a typo. They achieved this feat by literally hardwiring [is
that right word?] the model's weights into the silicon itself, so inference becomes
a matter of electrical signals flowing through physical transistors rather than loading
weights from memory. The big catch is, well, the chip is capable of only running
Llama 3.1 8B. If they want to use a different model, they need to design a completely
new chip. It's an extreme trade-off, but it shows just how fast these models can
run when the design for speed is done from the ground up.

### But Is faster always better?

For a lot of what people actually do with chat based AI, there's probably a
speed ceiling beyond which extra TPS stops mattering for the human on the other
end.

The average person reads somewhere around 250 words per minute. Let's say
roughly 330 tokens per minute, or about 5–6 tokens per second. At 50 TPS, the
model is already outrunning your eyes by nearly 10x. At 1,000 TPS, the response
essentially teleports [can we use harry potter phrase here?] in. This isn't a
complaint — it's genuinely nice when things are fast — but it raises a question:
who actually benefits from 15,000 tokens per second?

The answer, I think, is _not_ the person watching the chat window. It's the
**agent**.

This is the thread connecting Codex-Spark and Opus 4.6 Fast Mode. Both are
explicitly aimed at agentic coding workflows — you're watching, directing,
redirecting, catching mistakes in real-time rather than waiting ten minutes for
an autonomous run to finish. At 1,000 TPS the feedback cycle gets tight enough
to feel like collaboration rather than delegation. The impulse to pick up your
phone and scroll while you wait is removed.

But push further — to Taalas-scale speeds — and you're not designing for
human-readable output anymore. You're designing for AI-readable output. Agents
calling other agents, pipelines doing multi-step reasoning where intermediate
results feed forward, tasks that currently take an hour dropping to minutes
because every step is near instant.

A few things feel plausible to me, though I hold all of this loosely:

**Tighter human-AI loops in creative and coding work.** There's a real
difference between "paste my whole file in and wait" and "here, watch me think
through this line by line and stop me if I go wrong." Speed enables the second
mode in ways that latency-heavy models can't.

**Cheaper agentic pipelines.** Multi-step AI workflows burn a lot of tokens
waiting. If inference gets cheap _and_ fast enough, the economics of chaining
together ten model calls improve dramatically.

**New interaction modalities.** Real-time voice and video AI already push on
inference limits. As models get faster, you start to wonder what's possible when
the latency drops below the threshold of human perception entirely — where the
AI isn't streaming an answer so much as _being present_ in a conversation.

**The small-model renaissance.** Taalas runs a quantized 8B model, not a
frontier giant. There's an interesting counterpoint emerging to the "bigger is
always better" assumption: a well-optimized small model running at absurd speed
on purpose-built hardware might be more useful for many tasks than a 400B model
that takes two seconds to start talking. [Apparently they are aiming to
support much larger models soon so not sure this is a great point]

---

What I keep coming back to is that **speed is infrastructure**. We don't usually
praise electricity for being fast, but we notice immediately when it isn't.
Inference speed might be heading in the same direction — less of a feature, more
of a foundation that makes other things possible. The labs building for it now
are betting on that future arriving sooner than most people expect.
